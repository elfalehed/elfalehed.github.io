For a business to exist it needs to solve an issue people have. You sell value through a product or a service that a certain audience/niche need. Across very different political and economic systems the same patterns repeats, instituions has a clear mandate that it needs to respect be it: insurring health costs, delivering mail or even something as simple as issuing documents and for some structural reason it stops reliably delivering on it. Substrate treats this as a systems problems rather than a relationship problem. The goal is to make the alternative layer legible: mapped with evidence, described in a common structure, and backed by a trust record that travels with an operator instead of dying with a phone number.

In simple terms, Substrate map these failed mandates and where certain institutions have failed to deliver on their promise and through a structured and cleared out process, we help creating the alternative natively designed for that specific region/market. 

The main issue here isn't to simply find or map out the institutional failure, it's about creating a system through which optimized alternatives can exist in a healthy manner and offer something better for the locals. And to scale such an idea, we need to carefully study the operational and capital layer as to how to execute this and how to fund it. Substrate is going to be a part of an ecosystem build by young founders that want to diversify their investments. And with a cleared out data-driven approach, we evaluate each opportunity and what it takes to solve it on a scale. 

Here's an example of a document made after the heatwave that struck Tunisia this month:

# Substrate Entry: Tunisia — Electricity & Water Delivery Failure, July 2026

## 1. Trigger event

A heat wave beginning ~July 8, 2026 pushed temperatures 8–13°C above seasonal averages for
over a week, reaching close to 48°C in parts of the country, with the National Institute of
Meteorology placing 21 provinces under high alert. Electricity demand rose more than 30%
above normal, peaking around 5,000 MW/day between 2–4pm. STEG (Société Tunisienne de
l'Électricité et du Gaz) responded with rotating 30–45 minute load-shedding, a tool it has
now used in 2023, 2025, and 2026 — the 2023 heat wave produced a near-total nationwide
blackout after a breakdown at the Radès power station.

Algeria and Libya, Tunisia's main import partners for grid balancing, could not supply extra
capacity because both were experiencing the same heat wave. An additional technical fault at
a power plant in eastern Algeria further cut import volumes mid-crisis.

Water failed in parallel: SONEDE (Société Nationale d'Exploitation et de Distribution des
Eaux) attributes a large share of July's water outages directly to the electricity cuts,
since pumping stations cannot run without grid power — a cascading dependency between two
separate state mandates.

---

## 2. Mandate inventory

| Institution | Official mandate |
|---|---|
| **STEG** (state, electricity + gas) | Universal, reliable electricity supply at regulated tariffs |
| **SONEDE** (state, water) | Universal potable water distribution |
| Both | Continuity of service during demand or climate stress, per national energy/water strategy commitments (2026–2030 development plan, 2035 energy transition plan) |

Both are monopolies with no legal private alternative for last-mile delivery — the mandate
gap can only be filled *around* them, not by replacing them outright.

---

## 3. Failure mode classification

Substrate's five categories: liquidity/trust, capacity, regulatory friction, logistics,
governance. This event is a **compound failure** — rare, and higher-opportunity than a
single-mode failure because it supports alternatives at multiple points in the chain.

| Failure | Primary mode | Secondary mode |
|---|---|---|
| Electricity load-shedding | **Capacity** (generation + import capacity below peak demand) | **Liquidity** (STEG debt of 7.36bn TND, 6.06bn TND in unpaid receivables constrains new investment) |
| Water outages | **Logistics/infrastructure** (network losses ~25%, pipes 70+ years old, ~70 breaks/day) | **Governance** (cross-utility dependency never engineered around — SONEDE has no backup power plan for pumping stations) |
| Public information/accountability | **Governance** | — (rolling-cut schedules published but frequently not honored; unscheduled outages exceed the announced ones) |

Severity marker: this is not a nuisance-tier failure. Reported deaths from oxygen-dependent
patients losing power at home, and repeated damage to refrigerated stock in shops,
restaurants and cafés, put this at the same severity tier as an acute-care access failure —
which is unusual for a utility outage and materially changes the willingness-to-pay
calculation below.

---

## 4. Five-axis scoring

Scale 1 (low) – 5 (high).

| Axis | Electricity capacity failure | Water logistics failure |
|---|---|---|
| **Failure severity** | 5 — deaths, business losses, near-blackout precedent | 4 — no fatalities reported this cycle, but 650,000 rural residents already without running water, dams as low as ~17% in places |
| **Ability/willingness to pay** | 4 — SMEs, clinics, food retail, and mid-to-high income households have shown repeated willingness to pay for backup power after 2023/2025 precedent | 3 — willingness concentrated in commercial users and urban middle class; rural affected population has lower ability to pay |
| **Capital intensity to build an alternative** | Low at status/aggregation stage, high at owned-capacity (generation) stage | Low at status/trucking-concierge stage, very high at owned-capacity (desalination/network) stage |
| **Regulatory exposure** | Moderate — STEG holds generation/distribution monopoly, but distributed solar, batteries, and demand-response are legal and government-encouraged under the 2035 transition plan (target: renewables from ~5% to 30% of generation by 2030) | Moderate — water trucking and private tankering are already legal and operating informally; large-scale private distribution is not |
| **Relationship-dependence of existing informal layer** | Low-moderate — generator/battery suppliers already operate as formal retail businesses, not personal networks | High — water trucking today is largely informal, ad hoc, and relationship-based (who has a truck, who you call) |

**Read:** electricity is the more investable failure at scale (higher severity, higher
capacity to pay, government policy already pointed the same direction). Water is the more
legible Substrate opportunity in the strict sense — informal, opaque, relationship-based
alternatives already exist and are ripe for the "make it legible" treatment.

---

## 5. Alternative pairs, by build stage

Each pair follows Substrate's build order: **read-only status aggregation → paid concierge/
expediting → owned capacity.**

### 5.1 Electricity

**Stage 1 — Status aggregation (lowest liability, validates demand)**
- Real-time outage-map app aggregating STEG's published rotating-cut schedules against
  crowd-sourced actual outage reports (the gap between the two is itself the product — this
  week's reporting shows unscheduled outages regularly exceeding announced ones).
- SMS/WhatsApp outage alerts for households with medical equipment dependent on power — a
  direct, low-cost response to the oxygen-machine deaths, and a natural wedge into B2C trust.

**Stage 2 — Paid concierge / expediting**
- Business continuity subscription for SMEs: outage prediction + dispatchable backup
  (portable generator delivery, dispatched battery units) sold as a service, not equipment —
  addresses the reported spoilage of refrigerated stock in shops, restaurants and cafés
  without requiring the merchant to own a generator.
- Load-shifting-as-a-service for commercial/industrial users: managing consumption around
  STEG's 2–4pm peak in exchange for guaranteed continuity, monetizing the same demand-side
  behavior STEG is trying to induce anyway.
- Medical-grade backup power concierge for home healthcare (oxygen concentrators, dialysis,
  refrigerated medication) — small market, extremely high willingness to pay, direct
  life-safety framing.

**Stage 3 — Owned capacity (highest liability/capital, once demand is proven)**
- Distributed solar + battery mini-grids for commercial districts and industrial zones,
  explicitly aligned with the government's own 2026–2030 plan to expand rooftop solar and
  reach 30% renewable generation by 2030 — this is a case where Substrate's stated aim of
  "plugging into" the struggling institution rather than just replacing it is directly
  achievable, since STEG's own strategy document calls for private rooftop and efficiency
  investment it cannot itself fund given its debt load.
  - *Note:* government projections already put the addressable gap at roughly 600 MW of new
    generating capacity needed per year through 2030 — a public figure that functions as a
    ready-made market-sizing anchor for any generation-capacity pitch.
- C&I (commercial & industrial) battery storage fleets, financed against the demonstrated
  willingness of businesses to pay a premium to avoid the losses seen this week.

### 5.2 Water

**Stage 1 — Status aggregation**
- A single map/status layer combining SONEDE's cut schedules with the recurring root-cause
  data point that most water cuts trace back to power cuts at pumping stations — this
  causal link is not currently surfaced to the public in real time and is a genuine
  information gap Substrate's "map" methodology is built for.

**Stage 2 — Paid concierge**
- Formalized water trucking/tankering, converting today's informal, relationship-based
  supply into a bookable, rated, trust-scored service — the clearest single application of
  Substrate's "portable reputation ledger" concept in this entry, since the underlying
  informal layer already exists and is exactly the opaque, non-transferable kind Substrate
  is designed to formalize.
- Backup power specifically for critical SONEDE pumping infrastructure, sold to SONEDE or to
  large institutional/commercial water users as a resilience contract — a direct plug into
  the institution rather than a bypass of it.

**Stage 3 — Owned capacity**
- Leak-detection and network-rehabilitation contracting: SONEDE's own network loses roughly
  a quarter of the water it carries, with pipes over 70 years old failing at a reported rate
  of about 70 breaks per day — this is already a stated government priority (SONEDE's prior
  JICA-backed leak-detection cooperation is precedent) and represents the highest-capital,
  highest-impact stage.
- Small-scale desalination or greywater/wastewater reuse for commercial and agricultural
  users in the most water-stressed governorates, reducing draw on a dam system reported as
  low as ~17% in places.

---

## 6. Investment sizing, at scale

Order-of-magnitude framing based on figures reported this cycle, not a formal model:

- **Electricity generation gap:** government's own 600 MW/year additional-capacity target
  through 2030 is a public, citable market size for distributed generation and storage
  investment — roughly $500M–$1B+ in cumulative generation/storage capex over the period at
  typical solar+storage cost benchmarks, before financing and land costs.
- **STEG's balance sheet distress** (7.36bn TND debt, 6.06bn TND receivables) signals the
  state utility itself cannot fund this gap alone — the opening Substrate identifies for
  private/concierge capital to plug into a stressed institution rather than compete with it.
- **Water network losses** of ~25% imply that leak-detection and rehabilitation capex pays
  for itself largely through recovered non-revenue water, independent of climate risk —
  a lower-risk, infrastructure-grade investment thesis than backup generation.
- **Backup-power-as-a-service** (electricity Stage 2) is the fastest-to-revenue play: no
  license required beyond standard commercial operation, existing precedent from 2023/2025
  heat waves, and a demonstrated customer base (SMEs, food retail, home healthcare) with
  acute, recent, and repeatable pain.

---

## 7. Governance note

Per Substrate's founding constraint, none of the above is framed as replacing STEG or
SONEDE, evading Tunisian law, or building around regulation. The highest-return paths
identified here (distributed generation aligned with the 2035 transition plan; leak
detection aligned with SONEDE's own JICA precedent; resilience contracts sold *to* the
utilities) are explicitly the ones that plug into stated government strategy rather than
compete with the state mandate — consistent with Substrate's stated preference for alternatives
that struggling institutions can eventually plug into.
